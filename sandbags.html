STUDY FINDS 9 OUT OF 10 SANDBAGS USELESS BUT COMFORTING

VENICE, ITALY — When the acqua alta rolls in and Venice starts looking like a city built on water—because, well, it literally is—locals scramble to stack sandbags along narrow streets and shop entrances. But new research from the Institute of Semi-Effective Flood Defenses reveals a cold, wet truth: 9 out of 10 of those trusty sandbags do almost nothing to stop the floodwaters.

Lead researcher Dr. Sandy Bottom explained the findings at a press conference held just steps from a particularly soggy piazza. “The sandbags mainly just redirect the water temporarily or end up getting soggy and disintegrating. But here’s the kicker—they provide a huge psychological boost.”

Sandbags: Venice’s Emotional Lifeline

In Venice, where flooding is more frequent than a tourist selfie, sandbag stacking has become less about actual flood prevention and more about a communal ritual of hope and resilience. “We may know the water will come,” said local café owner Giulia Rossi, “but placing each bag feels like pushing back against the tide, even if it’s just a tiny push.”

The tradition has sparked a neighborhood phenomenon: entire families, friends, and neighbors turning sandbagging into a community event. There’s espresso passed around, stories shared, and intense debates over whether the bags should be stacked in a pyramid, wall, or some avant-garde shape inspired by modern art. “It’s like therapy with dirt and water,” laughed retiree Paolo Bianchi.

When Sandbags Fail, Venice Fights Back

While the study’s data was grim—showing water slipping past, pooling underneath, or simply flowing around sandbags like they weren’t even there—engineers and city planners stress that sandbags remain a symbol of resistance. “More advanced measures, like mobile flood barriers and elevated walkways, are necessary,” explained flood expert Lucia Verdi, “but those don’t replace the human spirit.”

Indeed, Venetians treat sandbags as a physical mantra: a way to show that no matter how high the waters rise, their spirit stands taller. The city has weathered floods for centuries, and while sandbags may be functionally useless, they’re anything but pointless.

Tourists, meanwhile, snap photos of these beige barricades as part of the authentic Venice experience. Some even mistake the bags for modern art installations—adding a splash of unintentional culture to the city’s flooded charm.

What This Means for Flood Preparedness

Experts urge residents worldwide not to rely solely on sandbags for protection but rather view them as one piece of a larger flood defense puzzle. But when the waves come crashing, having a wall of sandbags feels like a mental shield just as important as any physical barrier.

So next time you see a Venice street lined with sandbags, remember: they’re less about stopping the water and more about holding onto hope, community, and the kind of stubborn vibes that keep a city afloat—literally and figuratively.

Because if there’s one thing Venetians know, it’s that sometimes the best defense is just showing up and stacking up, bag by soggy bag.
